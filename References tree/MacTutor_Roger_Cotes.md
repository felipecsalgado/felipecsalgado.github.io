    <h3>Biography</h3>
    <span class="markup"><strong>Roger Cotes</strong>' mother was Grace Farmer, who came from Barwell in Leicestershire, and his father was Robert Cotes who was the rector of Burbage. Roger had a brother Anthony one year older than himself, and a sister Susanna who was one year younger. He attended Leicester School and by the age of twelve his teachers had already realised that he had an exceptional mathematical talent. His uncle, the Reverend John Smith, was keen to give Roger every chance to develop these talents and so Roger went to live with him so that he might be personally tutored. Roger later attended the famous St Paul's School in London, but he continued to be advised by his uncle and the two exchanged letters on mathematical topics during the time that Roger spent at school in London.
<br/>

<br/>
Roger matriculated at Trinity College, Cambridge, on <span class="non-italic">6</span> April <span class="non-italic">1699</span> as a pensioner, meaning that he did not have a scholarship and paid for his own keep in College. He graduated with a B.A. in <span class="non-italic">1702</span> and remained at Cambridge where he was elected to a fellowship in <span class="non-italic">1705</span>. In January <span class="non-italic">1706</span> he was nominated to be the first Plumian Professor of Astronomy and Experimental Philosophy. This was a remarkable achievement for Cotes who, at that time, was on <span class="non-italic">23</span> years of age. His exceptional abilities had been fully appreciated, however, by many at Cambridge such as <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Whiston/popup/" href="../Whiston/">William Whiston</a> with whom he had quickly formed a friendship. Both <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Newton/popup/" href="../Newton/">Newton</a> and <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Whiston/popup/" href="../Whiston/">Whiston</a> recommended Cotes for the Chair, as did Richard Bentley who was master of Trinity College. There were some, however, who opposed his appointment, the most high profile of whom was <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Flamsteed/popup/" href="../Flamsteed/">Flamsteed</a>, the astronomer royal. By the time that Cotes was formally elected as Plumian Professor on <span class="non-italic">16</span> October <span class="non-italic">1707</span> he had, in the previous year, been elected to a more prestigious fellowship as well as being awarded his M.A. Meli gives the background to the establishment of the chair in <span><span class="non-italic">[</span><a class="reference" data-popup='&lt;span class="markup"&gt;Biography by Domenico Bertoloni Meli, in &lt;em&gt;Dictionary of National Biography&lt;/em&gt; &lt;span class="non-italic"&gt;(&lt;/span&gt;Oxford, &lt;span class="non-italic"&gt;2004)&lt;/span&gt;.
&lt;br&gt;
&lt;br/&gt;
&lt;br&gt;
See &lt;a href="../../DNB/Cotes.html"&gt;THIS LINK&lt;/a&gt;.&lt;/span&gt;' href="#reference-2"><span class="non-italic">2</span></a><span class="non-italic">]</span></span>:-
<blockquote>Cotes was the first occupant of the Cambridge chair established by Thomas Plume <span class="non-italic">(1630</span> - <span class="non-italic">1704)</span>, archdeacon of Rochester, who bequeathed nearly £<span class="non-italic">2000</span> to maintain a professor and erect an astronomical observatory. Plans for an observatory at Trinity had already been drafted by Bentley before Plume's bequest. The observatory was eventually housed over the king's or great gate at Trinity College, together with living quarters for the Plumian professor.</blockquote>
It is not entirely clear how successful Cotes was in his role as an observational astronomer. In the first place there are somewhat contradictory accounts of the quality of the instruments in the Cambridge observatory. Cotes designed a transit telescope to add to a collection of instruments which had been purchased or donated. For example <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Newton/popup/" href="../Newton/">Newton</a> donated a clock which still survives at Trinity College. Bentley, the master of Trinity College we mentioned above, claimed that the Observatory had "the best instruments in Europe" but an assistant who worked there wrote to <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Flamsteed/popup/" href="../Flamsteed/">Flamsteed</a> saying "I saw nothing there that might deserve your notice". The truth is probably somewhere in between, since it would be natural for the master of Trinity to boast of the facilities, while the assistant, who only worked there for a short time, was probably trying to please <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Flamsteed/popup/" href="../Flamsteed/">Flamsteed</a>. In terms of the observations that Cotes made, perhaps the most significant was the total eclipse on <span class="non-italic">22</span> April <span class="non-italic">1715</span>. However, <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Halley/popup/" href="../Halley/">Halley</a> describes this event in a paper in the <em>Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society</em> where he says that Cotes:-
<blockquote>... had the misfortune to be opprest by too much company, so that though the heavens were very favourable, yet he missed both the times of the beginning of the eclipse and that of total darkness.</blockquote>
