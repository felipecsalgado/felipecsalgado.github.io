    <h3>Biography</h3>
    <span class="markup"><strong>Vincenzo Viviani</strong>'s father was Jacopo di Michelangelo Viviani and his mother was Maria Alamanno del Nente; both were members of a noble families. Vincenzo studied at a Jesuit school where he learnt the humanities. He was taught mathematics by Clemente Settimi, a Scolopian friar and a friend of <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Galileo/popup/" href="../Galileo/">Galileo</a>. Settimi quickly saw Viviani's exceptional intelligence and, in <span class="non-italic">1638</span>, presented him at court to Ferdinando II de' Medici, Grand Duke of Tuscany. The court was in Livorno, so Viviani had to make the long journey from Florence. While on the road he did not waste his time but spent the many hours studying the first three books of <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Euclid/popup/" href="../Euclid/">Euclid</a>'s <em>Elements</em>. He made a presentation to the Tuscan Court explaining the first sixteen propositions in the first book of the <em>Elements</em>. Famiano Michelini, Ferdinando's Court mathematician, then set him a problem to which he responded confidently. Ferdinando was greatly impressed and provided a monthly salary for Viviani that enabled him to purchase mathematical books. He also arranged for Viviani to meet <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Galileo/popup/" href="../Galileo/">Galileo</a>, who was living in his villa in Arcetri, near Florence, where he had been put under house arrest by the Catholic Church. Despite the house arrest, <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Galileo/popup/" href="../Galileo/">Galileo</a> was still Ferdinando's Tuscan Court Mathematician.
<br/>

<br/>
<a class="mlink" data-popup="../Galileo/popup/" href="../Galileo/">Galileo</a>, who by this time was totally blind, was very impressed by Viviani's knowledge and abilities. In <span class="non-italic">1639</span>, he took Viviani into his home as a companion, student and collaborator, and Viviani continued in this role until <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Galileo/popup/" href="../Galileo/">Galileo</a> died in January <span class="non-italic">1642</span>. Viviani learnt much from <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Galileo/popup/" href="../Galileo/">Galileo</a> over this period, working with him on physics and geometry. However, as Viviani relates himself, their relationship went well beyond that of scientist and assistant, becoming much more like that of a father and son. He wrote <span class="non-italic">(</span>see for example <span><span class="non-italic">[</span><a class="reference" data-popup='&lt;span class="markup"&gt;L Boschiero, Post-Galilean Thought and Experiment in Seventeenth-century Italy: The Life and Work of Vincenzio Viviani, &lt;em&gt;History of Science &lt;/em&gt;&lt;strong&gt;&lt;span class="non-italic"&gt;43&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/strong&gt; &lt;span class="non-italic"&gt;(2005)&lt;/span&gt;, &lt;span class="non-italic"&gt;77&lt;/span&gt;-&lt;span class="non-italic"&gt;100&lt;/span&gt;.&lt;/span&gt;' href="#reference-4"><span class="non-italic">4</span></a><span class="non-italic">]</span></span><span class="non-italic">)</span>:-
<blockquote>Soon after the unexpected publication of 'Discourses and mathematical demonstrations concerning the two new sciences', Signor <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Galileo/popup/" href="../Galileo/">Galileo</a> allowed me into his villa in Arcetri where he was staying. I was able to benefit from our intelligent conversations and his precious teachings and he was content that in the study of mathematics, which I had only recently begun, I could turn to his own voice for the solution of those doubts and difficulties that I often found through the natural weakness of my intellect.</blockquote>
In October <span class="non-italic">1641</span>, <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Galileo/popup/" href="../Galileo/">Galileo</a> and Viviani were joined in the villa at Arcetri by <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Torricelli/popup/" href="../Torricelli/">Evangelista Torricelli</a> when he moved from Rome. After <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Galileo/popup/" href="../Galileo/">Galileo</a>'s death, <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Torricelli/popup/" href="../Torricelli/">Torricelli</a> was appointed to fill <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Galileo/popup/" href="../Galileo/">Galileo</a>'s post as Ferdinando II de' Medici's Tuscan Court Mathematician and Viviani continued his collaboration with him. <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Torricelli/popup/" href="../Torricelli/">Torricelli</a> is most famed as the first person to create a sustained vacuum and to discover the principle of a barometer. In <span class="non-italic">1643</span> <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Torricelli/popup/" href="../Torricelli/">Torricelli</a> proposed an experiment which would demonstrate that atmospheric pressure determines the height to which a fluid will rise in a tube filled with a liquid, then inverted over the same liquid. In <span class="non-italic">1644</span> Viviani, as <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Torricelli/popup/" href="../Torricelli/">Torricelli</a>'s collaborator, carried out the experiment which proved a major scientific advance and led to the development of the barometer. When <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Torricelli/popup/" href="../Torricelli/">Torricelli</a> died in <span class="non-italic">1647</span>, Viviani was appointed to fill the lectureship at the Accademia del Disegno in Florence, holding this post for two years. The Grand Duke also appointed Viviani as mathematics teacher to the Medici family at Court, and as engineer with the Uffiziali dei Fiumi, a position he held for the rest of his life <span><span class="non-italic">[</span><a class="reference" data-popup='&lt;span class="markup"&gt;L Boschiero, Post-Galilean Thought and Experiment in Seventeenth-century Italy: The Life and Work of Vincenzio Viviani, &lt;em&gt;History of Science &lt;/em&gt;&lt;strong&gt;&lt;span class="non-italic"&gt;43&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/strong&gt; &lt;span class="non-italic"&gt;(2005)&lt;/span&gt;, &lt;span class="non-italic"&gt;77&lt;/span&gt;-&lt;span class="non-italic"&gt;100&lt;/span&gt;.&lt;/span&gt;' href="#reference-4"><span class="non-italic">4</span></a><span class="non-italic">]</span></span>:-
<blockquote>While his responsibilities as a Court Mathematician often varied according to the desires of the Grand Duke, the travels of the Tuscan Court, and the arrival on the scene of other thinkers, all his life he was kept busy by his duties to the Medici Court. Particularly exhausting for him was his role of engineer, which required substantial travel on horseback and led to illness on more than one occasion.</blockquote>
The main thrust of Viviani's life, however, was to keep <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Galileo/popup/" href="../Galileo/">Galileo</a>'s memory alive and he wanted to do so by publishing his collected works.
