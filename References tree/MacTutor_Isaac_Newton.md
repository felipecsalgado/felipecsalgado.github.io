    <h3>Biography</h3>
    <span class="markup"><strong>Isaac Newton</strong>'s life can be divided into three quite distinct periods. The first is his boyhood days from <span class="non-italic">1643</span> up to his appointment to a chair in <span class="non-italic">1669</span>. The second period from <span class="non-italic">1669</span> to <span class="non-italic">1687</span> was the highly productive period in which he was Lucasian professor at Cambridge. The third period <span class="non-italic">(</span>nearly as long as the other two combined<span class="non-italic">)</span> saw Newton as a highly paid government official in London with little further interest in mathematical research.
<br/>

<br/>
Isaac Newton was born in the manor house of Woolsthorpe, near Grantham in Lincolnshire. Although by the calendar in use at the time of his birth he was born on Christmas Day <span class="non-italic">1642</span>, we give the date of <span class="non-italic">4</span> January <span class="non-italic">1643</span> in this biography which is the "corrected" Gregorian calendar date bringing it into line with our present calendar. <span class="non-italic">(</span>The Gregorian calendar was not adopted in England until <span class="non-italic">1752</span>.<span class="non-italic">)</span> Isaac Newton came from a family of farmers but never knew his father, also named Isaac Newton, who died in October <span class="non-italic">1642</span>, three months before his son was born. Although Isaac's father owned property and animals which made him quite a wealthy man, he was completely uneducated and could not sign his own name.
<br/>

<br/>
You can see a picture of Woolsthorpe Manor as it is now at <a class="tippyPic" href="../../Diagrams/Woolsthorpe.jpeg">THIS LINK</a>.
<br/>

<br/>
Isaac's mother Hannah Ayscough remarried Barnabas Smith the minister of the church at North Witham, a nearby village, when Isaac was two years old. The young child was then left in the care of his grandmother Margery Ayscough at Woolsthorpe. Basically treated as an orphan, Isaac did not have a happy childhood. His grandfather James Ayscough was never mentioned by Isaac in later life and the fact that James left nothing to Isaac in his will, made when the boy was ten years old, suggests that there was no love lost between the two. There is no doubt that Isaac felt very bitter towards his mother and his step-father Barnabas Smith. When examining his sins at age nineteen, Isaac listed:-
<blockquote>Threatening my father and mother Smith to burn them and the house over them.</blockquote>
Upon the death of his stepfather in <span class="non-italic">1653</span>, Newton lived in an extended family consisting of his mother, his grandmother, one half-brother, and two half-sisters. From shortly after this time Isaac began attending the Free Grammar School in Grantham. Although this was only five miles from his home, Isaac lodged with the Clark family at Grantham. However he seems to have shown little promise in academic work. His school reports described him as 'idle' and 'inattentive'. His mother, by now a lady of reasonable wealth and property, thought that her eldest son was the right person to manage her affairs and her estate. Isaac was taken away from school but soon showed that he had no talent, or interest, in managing an estate.
<br/>

<br/>
An uncle, William Ayscough, decided that Isaac should prepare for entering university and, having persuaded his mother that this was the right thing to do, Isaac was allowed to return to the Free Grammar School in Grantham in <span class="non-italic">1660</span> to complete his school education. This time he lodged with Stokes, who was the headmaster of the school, and it would appear that, despite suggestions that he had previously shown no academic promise, Isaac must have convinced some of those around him that he had academic promise. Some evidence points to Stokes also persuading Isaac's mother to let him enter university, so it is likely that Isaac had shown more promise in his first spell at the school than the school reports suggest. Another piece of evidence comes from Isaac's list of sins referred to above. He lists one of his sins as:-
<blockquote>... setting my heart on money, learning, and pleasure more than Thee ...</blockquote>
which tells us that Isaac must have had a passion for learning.
<br/>

<br/>
We know nothing about what Isaac learnt in preparation for university, but Stokes was an able man and almost certainly gave Isaac private coaching and a good grounding. There is no evidence that he learnt any mathematics, but we cannot rule out Stokes introducing him to <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Euclid/popup/" href="../Euclid/">Euclid</a>'s <em>Elements</em> which he was well capable of teaching <span class="non-italic">(</span>although there is evidence mentioned below that Newton did not read <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Euclid/popup/" href="../Euclid/">Euclid</a> before <span class="non-italic">1663)</span>. Anecdotes abound about a mechanical ability which Isaac displayed at the school and stories are told of his skill in making models of machines, in particular of clocks and windmills. However, when biographers seek information about famous people there is always a tendency for people to report what they think is expected of them, and these anecdotes may simply be made up later by those who felt that the most famous scientist in the world ought to have had these skills at school.
<br/>

<br/>
Newton entered his uncle's old College, Trinity College Cambridge, on <span class="non-italic">5</span> June <span class="non-italic">1661</span>. He was older than most of his fellow students but, despite the fact that his mother was financially well off, he entered as a <a class="gllink" data-popup="../../Glossary/sizar/" href="../../Glossary/#sizar">sizar</a>. A sizar at Cambridge was a student who received an allowance toward college expenses in exchange for acting as a servant to other students. There is certainly some ambiguity in his position as a sizar, for he seems to have associated with "better class" students rather than other sizars. Westfall <span class="non-italic">(</span>see <span><span class="non-italic">[</span><a class="reference" data-popup='&lt;span class="markup"&gt;R S Westfall, &lt;em&gt;Never at Rest: A Biography of Isaac Newton&lt;/em&gt; &lt;span class="non-italic"&gt;(1990)&lt;/span&gt;.&lt;/span&gt;' href="#reference-23"><span class="non-italic">23</span></a><span class="non-italic">]</span></span> or <span><span class="non-italic">[</span><a class="reference" data-popup='&lt;span class="markup"&gt;R S Westfall, &lt;em&gt;The Life of Isaac Newton&lt;/em&gt; &lt;span class="non-italic"&gt;(&lt;/span&gt;Cambridge, &lt;span class="non-italic"&gt;1993)&lt;/span&gt;.&lt;/span&gt;' href="#reference-24"><span class="non-italic">24</span></a><span class="non-italic">]</span></span><span class="non-italic">)</span> has suggested that Newton may have had Humphrey Babington, a distant relative who was a Fellow of Trinity, as his patron. This reasonable explanation would fit well with what is known and mean that his mother did not subject him unnecessarily to hardship as some of his biographers claim.
<br/>

<br/>
Newton's aim at Cambridge was a law degree. Instruction at Cambridge was dominated by the philosophy of <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Aristotle/popup/" href="../Aristotle/">Aristotle</a> but some freedom of study was allowed in the third year of the course. Newton studied the philosophy of <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Descartes/popup/" href="../Descartes/">Descartes</a>, <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Gassendi/popup/" href="../Gassendi/">Gassendi</a>, <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Hobbes/popup/" href="../Hobbes/">Hobbes</a>, and in particular <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Boyle/popup/" href="../Boyle/">Boyle</a>. The mechanics of the Copernican astronomy of <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Galileo/popup/" href="../Galileo/">Galileo</a> attracted him and he also studied <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Kepler/popup/" href="../Kepler/">Kepler</a>'s <em>Optics</em>. He recorded his thoughts in a book which he entitled <em>Quaestiones Quaedam Philosophicae</em> <span><a class="translation nonoscript non-italic" data-popup='&lt;span class="markup"&gt;Certain philosophical questions&lt;/span&gt;'>Ⓣ</a><noscript><span class="non-italic">(</span><span class="markup">Certain philosophical questions</span><span class="non-italic">)</span></noscript></span>. It is a fascinating account of how Newton's ideas were already forming around <span class="non-italic">1664</span>. He headed the text with a Latin statement meaning "<a class="mlink" data-popup="../Plato/popup/" href="../Plato/">Plato</a> is my friend, <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Aristotle/popup/" href="../Aristotle/">Aristotle</a> is my friend, but my best friend is truth" showing himself a free thinker from an early stage.
<br/>

<br/>
How Newton was introduced to the most advanced mathematical texts of his day is slightly less clear. According to <a class="mlink" data-popup="../De_Moivre/popup/" href="../De_Moivre/">de Moivre</a>, Newton's interest in mathematics began in the autumn of <span class="non-italic">1663</span> when he bought an astrology book at a fair in Cambridge and found that he could not understand the mathematics in it. Attempting to read a trigonometry book, he found that he lacked knowledge of geometry and so decided to read <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Barrow/popup/" href="../Barrow/">Barrow</a>'s edition of <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Euclid/popup/" href="../Euclid/">Euclid</a>'s <em>Elements</em>. The first few results were so easy that he almost gave up but he:-
<blockquote>... changed his mind when he read that parallelograms upon the same base and between the same parallels are equal.</blockquote>
Returning to the beginning, Newton read the whole book with a new respect. He then turned to <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Oughtred/popup/" href="../Oughtred/">Oughtred</a>'s <em>Clavis Mathematica</em> <span><a class="translation nonoscript non-italic" data-popup='&lt;span class="markup"&gt;The key of  mathematics&lt;/span&gt;'>Ⓣ</a><noscript><span class="non-italic">(</span><span class="markup">The key of  mathematics</span><span class="non-italic">)</span></noscript></span> and <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Descartes/popup/" href="../Descartes/">Descartes</a>' <em>La Géométrie</em> <span><a class="translation nonoscript non-italic" data-popup='&lt;span class="markup"&gt;Geometry&lt;/span&gt;'>Ⓣ</a><noscript><span class="non-italic">(</span><span class="markup">Geometry</span><span class="non-italic">)</span></noscript></span>. The new algebra and analytical geometry of <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Viete/popup/" href="../Viete/">Viète</a> was read by Newton from <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Schooten/popup/" href="../Schooten/">Frans van Schooten</a>'s edition of <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Viete/popup/" href="../Viete/">Viète</a>'s collected works published in <span class="non-italic">1646</span>. Other major works of mathematics which he studied around this time was the newly published major work by <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Schooten/popup/" href="../Schooten/">van Schooten</a> <em>Geometria a Renato Des Cartes</em> <span><a class="translation nonoscript non-italic" data-popup='&lt;span class="markup"&gt;Geometry of Descartes&lt;/span&gt;'>Ⓣ</a><noscript><span class="non-italic">(</span><span class="markup">Geometry of Descartes</span><span class="non-italic">)</span></noscript></span> which appeared in two volumes in <span class="non-italic">1659</span>-<span class="non-italic">1661</span>. The book contained important appendices by three of <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Schooten/popup/" href="../Schooten/">van Schooten</a>'s disciples, <a class="mlink" data-popup="../De_Witt/popup/" href="../De_Witt/">Jan de Witt</a>, <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Hudde/popup/" href="../Hudde/">Johan Hudde</a>, and <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Heuraet/popup/" href="../Heuraet/">Hendrick van Heuraet</a>. Newton also studied <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Wallis/popup/" href="../Wallis/">Wallis</a>'s <em>Algebra</em> and it appears that his first original mathematical work came from his study of this text. He read <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Wallis/popup/" href="../Wallis/">Wallis</a>'s method for finding a square of equal area to a <a class="gllink" data-popup="../../Glossary/parabola/" href="../../Glossary/#parabola">parabola</a> and a <a class="gllink" data-popup="../../Glossary/hyperbola/" href="../../Glossary/#hyperbola">hyperbola</a> which used indivisibles. Newton made notes on <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Wallis/popup/" href="../Wallis/">Wallis</a>'s treatment of series but also devised his own proofs of the theorems writing:-
<blockquote>Thus <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Wallis/popup/" href="../Wallis/">Wallis</a> doth it, but it may be done thus ...</blockquote>
It would be easy to think that Newton's talent began to emerge on the arrival of <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Barrow/popup/" href="../Barrow/">Barrow</a> to the Lucasian chair at Cambridge in <span class="non-italic">1663</span> when he became a Fellow at Trinity College. Certainly the date matches the beginnings of Newton's deep mathematical studies. However, it would appear that the <span class="non-italic">1663</span> date is merely a coincidence and that it was only some years later that <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Barrow/popup/" href="../Barrow/">Barrow</a> recognised the mathematical genius among his students.
<br/>

<br/>
Despite some evidence that his progress had not been particularly good, Newton was elected a scholar on <span class="non-italic">28</span> April <span class="non-italic">1664</span> and received his bachelor's degree in April <span class="non-italic">1665</span>. It would appear that his scientific genius had still not emerged, but it did so suddenly when the plague closed the University in the summer of <span class="non-italic">1665</span> and he had to return to Lincolnshire. There, in a period of less than two years, while Newton was still under <span class="non-italic">25</span> years old, he began revolutionary advances in mathematics, optics, physics, and astronomy.
<br/>

<br/>
While Newton remained at home he laid the foundations for differential and integral calculus, several years before its independent discovery by <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Leibniz/popup/" href="../Leibniz/">Leibniz</a>. The 'method of fluxions', as he termed it, was based on his crucial insight that the integration of a function is merely the inverse procedure to differentiating it. Taking differentiation as the basic operation, Newton produced simple analytical methods that unified many separate techniques previously developed to solve apparently unrelated problems such as finding areas, <a class="gllink" data-popup="../../Glossary/tangent/" href="../../Glossary/#tangent">tangents</a>, the lengths of curves and the maxima and minima of functions. Newton's <em>De Methodis Serierum et Fluxionum</em> <span><a class="translation nonoscript non-italic" data-popup='&lt;span class="markup"&gt;On methods of series and on fluxions&lt;/span&gt;'>Ⓣ</a><noscript><span class="non-italic">(</span><span class="markup">On methods of series and on fluxions</span><span class="non-italic">)</span></noscript></span> was written in <span class="non-italic">1671</span> but Newton failed to get it published and it did not appear in print until John Colson produced an English translation in <span class="non-italic">1736</span>.
<br/>

<br/>
When the University of Cambridge reopened after the plague in <span class="non-italic">1667</span>, Newton put himself forward as a candidate for a fellowship. In October he was elected to a minor fellowship at Trinity College but, after being awarded his Master's Degree, he was elected to a major fellowship in July <span class="non-italic">1668</span> which allowed him to dine at the Fellows' Table. In July <span class="non-italic">1669</span> <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Barrow/popup/" href="../Barrow/">Barrow</a> tried to ensure that Newton's mathematical achievements became known to the world. He sent Newton's text <em>De Analysi</em> to <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Collins/popup/" href="../Collins/">Collins</a> in London writing:-
<blockquote><span class="non-italic">[</span><a class="mlink" data-popup="popup/" href="./">Newton</a><span class="non-italic">]</span> brought me the other day some papers, wherein he set down methods of calculating the dimensions of magnitudes like that of Mr <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Mercator_Nicolaus/popup/" href="../Mercator_Nicolaus/">Mercator</a> concerning the hyperbola, but very general; as also of resolving equations; which I suppose will please you; and I shall send you them by the next.</blockquote>
<a class="mlink" data-popup="../Collins/popup/" href="../Collins/">Collins</a> corresponded with all the leading mathematicians of the day so <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Barrow/popup/" href="../Barrow/">Barrow</a>'s action should have led to quick recognition. <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Collins/popup/" href="../Collins/">Collins</a> showed <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Brouncker/popup/" href="../Brouncker/">Brouncker</a>, the President of the <a class="aclink" href="../../Societies/RS/" rel="noreferrer" target="_blank">Royal Society</a>, Newton's results <span class="non-italic">(</span>with the author's permission<span class="non-italic">)</span> but after this Newton requested that his manuscript be returned. <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Collins/popup/" href="../Collins/">Collins</a> could not give a detailed account but <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Sluze/popup/" href="../Sluze/">de Sluze</a> and <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Gregory/popup/" href="../Gregory/">Gregory</a> learnt something of Newton's work through <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Collins/popup/" href="../Collins/">Collins</a>. <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Barrow/popup/" href="../Barrow/">Barrow</a> resigned the Lucasian chair in <span class="non-italic">1669</span> to devote himself to divinity, recommending that Newton <span class="non-italic">(</span>still only <span class="non-italic">27</span> years old<span class="non-italic">)</span> be appointed in his place. Shortly after this Newton visited London and twice met with <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Collins/popup/" href="../Collins/">Collins</a> but, as he wrote to <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Gregory/popup/" href="../Gregory/">Gregory</a>:-
<blockquote>... having no more acquaintance with him I did not think it becoming to urge him to communicate anything.</blockquote>
Newton's first work as Lucasian Professor was on optics and this was the topic of his first lecture course begun in January <span class="non-italic">1670</span>. He had reached the conclusion during the two plague years that white light is not a simple entity. Every scientist since <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Aristotle/popup/" href="../Aristotle/">Aristotle</a> had believed that white light was a basic single entity, but the chromatic aberration in a telescope lens convinced Newton otherwise. When he passed a thin beam of sunlight through a glass prism Newton noted the spectrum of colours that was formed.
<br/>

<br/>
He argued that white light is really a mixture of many different types of rays which are refracted at slightly different angles, and that each different type of ray produces a different spectral colour. Newton was led by this reasoning to the erroneous conclusion that telescopes using refracting lenses would always suffer chromatic aberration. He therefore proposed and constructed a reflecting telescope.
<br/>

<br/>
In <span class="non-italic">1672</span> Newton was elected a fellow of the <a class="aclink" href="../../Societies/RS/" rel="noreferrer" target="_blank">Royal Society</a> after donating a reflecting telescope. Also in <span class="non-italic">1672</span> Newton published his first scientific paper on light and colour in the <em>Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society</em>. The paper was generally well received but <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Hooke/popup/" href="../Hooke/">Hooke</a> and <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Huygens/popup/" href="../Huygens/">Huygens</a> objected to Newton's attempt to prove, by experiment alone, that light consists of the motion of small particles rather than waves. The reception that his publication received did nothing to improve Newton's attitude to making his results known to the world. He was always pulled in two directions, there was something in his nature which wanted fame and recognition yet another side of him feared criticism and the easiest way to avoid being criticised was to publish nothing. Certainly one could say that his reaction to criticism was irrational, and certainly his aim to humiliate <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Hooke/popup/" href="../Hooke/">Hooke</a> in public because of his opinions was abnormal. However, perhaps because of Newton's already high reputation, his corpuscular theory reigned until the wave theory was revived in the <span class="non-italic">19</span><span class="superscript">th</span>  century.
<br/>

<br/>
Newton's relations with <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Hooke/popup/" href="../Hooke/">Hooke</a> deteriorated further when, in <span class="non-italic">1675</span>, <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Hooke/popup/" href="../Hooke/">Hooke</a> claimed that Newton had stolen some of his optical results. Although the two men made their peace with an exchange of polite letters, Newton turned in on himself and away from the <a class="aclink" href="../../Societies/RS/" rel="noreferrer" target="_blank">Royal Society</a> which he associated with <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Hooke/popup/" href="../Hooke/">Hooke</a> as one of its leaders. He delayed the publication of a full account of his optical researches until after the death of <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Hooke/popup/" href="../Hooke/">Hooke</a> in <span class="non-italic">1703</span>. Newton's <em>Opticks</em> appeared in <span class="non-italic">1704</span>. It dealt with the theory of light and colour and with

To explain some of his observations he had to use a wave theory of light in conjunction with his corpuscular theory.
<br/>

<br/>
Another argument, this time with the English Jesuits in Liège over his theory of colour, led to a violent exchange of letters, then in <span class="non-italic">1678</span> Newton appears to have suffered a nervous breakdown. His mother died in the following year and he withdrew further into his shell, mixing as little as possible with people for a number of years.
<br/>

<br/>
Newton's greatest achievement was his work in physics and celestial mechanics, which culminated in the theory of universal gravitation. By <span class="non-italic">1666</span> Newton had early versions of his three laws of motion. He had also discovered the law giving the centrifugal force on a body moving uniformly in a circular path. However he did not have a correct understanding of the mechanics of circular motion.
<br/>

<br/>
Newton's novel idea of <span class="non-italic">1666</span> was to imagine that the Earth's gravity influenced the Moon, counter- balancing its centrifugal force. From his law of centrifugal force and <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Kepler/popup/" href="../Kepler/">Kepler</a>'s third law of planetary motion, Newton deduced the inverse-square law.
<br/>

<br/>
In <span class="non-italic">1679</span> Newton corresponded with <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Hooke/popup/" href="../Hooke/">Hooke</a> who had written to Newton claiming:-
<blockquote>... that the Attraction always is in a duplicate proportion to the Distance from the Center Reciprocall ...</blockquote>
M Nauenberg writes an account of the next events:-
<blockquote>After his <span class="non-italic">1679</span> correspondence with <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Hooke/popup/" href="../Hooke/">Hooke</a>, Newton, by his own account, found a proof that Kepler's areal law was a consequence of centripetal forces, and he also showed that if the orbital curve is an <a class="gllink" data-popup="../../Glossary/ellipse/" href="../../Glossary/#ellipse">ellipse</a> under the action of central forces then the radial dependence of the force is inverse square with the distance from the centre.</blockquote>
This discovery showed the physical significance of <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Kepler/popup/" href="../Kepler/">Kepler</a>'s second law.
<br/>

<br/>
In <span class="non-italic">1684</span> <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Halley/popup/" href="../Halley/">Halley</a>, tired of <a class="mlink" data-popup="../Hooke/popup/" href="../Hooke/">Hooke</a>'s boasting <span class="non-italic">[</span>M Nauenberg<span class="non-italic">]</span>:-
<blockquote>... asked Newton what orbit a body followed under an inverse square force, and Newton replied immediately that it would be an ellipse. However in 'De Motu..' he only gave a proof of the converse theorem that if the orbit is an ellipse the force is inverse square. The proof that inverse square forces imply <a class="gllink" data-popup="../../Glossary/conic/" href="../../Glossary/#conic">conic section</a> orbits is sketched in Cor. <span class="non-italic">1</span> to Prop. <span class="non-italic">13</span> in Book <span class="non-italic">1</span> of the second and third editions of the 'Principia', but not in the first edition.</blockquote>
<a class="mlink" data-popup="../Halley/popup/" href="../Halley/">Halley</a> persuaded Newton to write a full treatment of his new physics and its application to astronomy. Over a year later <span class="non-italic">(1687)</span> Newton published the <em>Philosophiae naturalis principia mathematica</em> <span><a class="translation nonoscript non-italic" data-popup='&lt;span class="markup"&gt;Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy&lt;/span&gt;'>Ⓣ</a><noscript><span class="non-italic">(</span><span class="markup">Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy</span><span class="non-italic">)</span></noscript></span> or <em>Principia</em> as it is always known.
<br/>

<br/>
The <em>Principia</em> is recognised as the greatest scientific book ever written. Newton analysed the motion of bodies in resisting and non-resisting media under the action of centripetal forces. The results were applied to orbiting bodies, projectiles, pendulums, and free-fall near the Earth.
